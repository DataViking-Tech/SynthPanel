# synth-panel 0.5.0 — CPO Vision

**Author:** synth-panel/cpo | **Date:** 2026-04-06

---

## What 0.4.0 Delivered (and What It Changed)

0.4.0 was the conversation release. It turned a single-shot panel into a multi-round research arc:

| Feature | Impact |
|---------|--------|
| Multi-round instruments | Round N synthesis templates round N+1 questions; same persona sessions reused across rounds |
| `response_sentiment` condition | Completes the conditional follow-up system from 0.3.0 (now: `response_contains`, `response_sentiment`, `always`, `never`) |
| Panel resume / extend | Agents can iterate on a panel through repeated tool calls without losing persona context |
| Backward-compatible output | Single-round instruments still emit the flat `results`/`synthesis` shape; multi-round emits `rounds` |

Multi-round was the inflection. Before it, synth-panel was a parallel-prompt-runner with a synthesis on top. After it, synth-panel is a research orchestrator: it sequences questions, carries persona memory, and shapes later questions from earlier findings. The agent surface inherited this directly — `extend_panel` made the MCP tool genuinely conversational, not just call-and-response.

But 0.4.0 also exposed the next ceiling: **every multi-round instrument is linear.** Round 1 → Round 2 → Round 3, regardless of what Round 1 found. If exploration surfaces a pricing objection, the instrument can template the *wording* of the probe round around it — but it can't choose a *different probe round* based on what was surfaced. The research arc is fixed at authoring time.

Real research isn't linear. A skilled interviewer hears something surprising and pivots — "we were going to talk about onboarding next, but you mentioned trust issues, let's dig there first." 0.4.0 can't do that. 0.5.0 closes the gap.

---

## The 0.5.0 Question

The original roadmap said 0.5.0 was "branching instruments (conditional round routing), conversation trees, instrument library."

**Is branching still the right bet?** Yes — and the framing tightens now that linear multi-round has shipped. The original framing implied a continuum from "linear rounds → branching → trees." That's wrong. Branching is a step change: it makes instruments **adaptive** to what the panel says, not just **templated** with what the panel said. That distinction is the entire 0.5.0 thesis.

There's also a second bet that the original roadmap underweighted: the **instrument library**. We have community-contributable persona packs (the infrastructure shipped in 0.2.0). We do not yet have an analogous mechanism for instruments. Instruments are the harder authoring problem — designing a good multi-round study is real research craft. Letting users share instruments is the second flywheel, parallel to persona packs. 0.5.0 should ship both.

## 0.5.0 Identity: The Adaptive Release

### The Headline: Branching Instruments

A branching instrument defines round routing as a function of the prior round's synthesis:

```yaml
instrument:
  version: 3
  rounds:
    - name: exploration
      questions:
        - text: "What frustrates you about project management tools?"
        - text: "How do you currently track work across teams?"

    - name: probe
      depends_on: exploration
      route_when:
        - if: "synthesis.themes contains 'pricing'"
          goto: probe_pricing
        - if: "synthesis.themes contains 'integrations'"
          goto: probe_integrations
        - else: probe_general

    - name: probe_pricing
      questions:
        - text: "Walk me through the last time you evaluated a tool's price."
        - text: "What would the price have to be for you to switch?"

    - name: probe_integrations
      questions:
        - text: "Which integrations are non-negotiable?"
        - text: "Where does your current tool's integration story break down?"

    - name: probe_general
      questions:
        - text: "Tell me about the last tool you abandoned. What made you stop?"

    - name: validation
      depends_on: [probe_pricing, probe_integrations, probe_general]
      questions:
        - text: "If a tool addressed {synthesis.recommendation}, would you switch?"
```

**Why this matters:** Linear multi-round is impressive but it's still a script. Branching turns the instrument into a decision tree where the *path* is data-driven. That makes synth-panel genuinely closer to how a researcher actually works — and meaningfully harder for "I could just prompt ChatGPT with personas" to replicate. ChatGPT can simulate one path. Synth-panel chooses the path based on what the panel actually said.

**Why this matters for agents:** An agent calling `run_panel` with a branching instrument gets even more leverage per tool call. Instead of "run this 3-round study," the call becomes "run this study, follow the right branch automatically, give me the synthesis at the end of whichever path the panel surfaced." The agent is offloading not just orchestration but **research-design judgment** to the harness. That is the agent-native value proposition deepening, not broadening — more decisions made inside one tool call.

### Three Features, Priority Order

#### 1. Branching Instruments (the headline)

The orchestrator gains a router. Before each round, the router evaluates `route_when` clauses against the latest synthesis and picks the next round. Rounds become a directed acyclic graph (DAG), not a list.

**Key design decisions (CPO calls — locked unless architect surfaces a blocker):**

- **DAG, not arbitrary graph.** No back-edges, no loops, no "go back to round 1." Each round runs at most once. This keeps cost bounded and makes audits tractable. If users want loops, that's a future release and a different feature.
- **Routing predicates use the same template-context vocabulary as 0.4.0.** `synthesis.themes`, `synthesis.recommendation`, `synthesis.disagreements`, etc. We do not invent a new expression language. Predicates: `contains`, `equals`, `matches` (regex), and a final `else`. That's it. Boring, auditable, no eval.
- **All rounds defined up front in the instrument.** No round generated at runtime. The whole DAG is visible to anyone reading the YAML — that's a research-reproducibility property, not a usability nicety.
- **Path is recorded in the result.** The output names which rounds ran and which routing predicate fired at each branch. Audits and reproducibility depend on this.
- **Linear multi-round (0.4.0 v2 instruments) keeps working.** v2 instruments are a degenerate v3 — implicit linear edges. No breaking change.

**Result shape addition:**
```json
{
  "rounds": [...],
  "path": [
    { "round": "exploration" },
    { "round": "probe", "branch": "synthesis.themes contains 'pricing'", "next": "probe_pricing" },
    { "round": "probe_pricing" },
    { "round": "validation" }
  ],
  "final_synthesis": {...}
}
```

#### 2. Instrument Library (the ecosystem play)

We already have persona packs (0.2.0). Add **instrument packs** as the second contributable artifact type.

```bash
# Browse community instrument packs
synth-panel instruments list

# Install
synth-panel instruments install pricing-discovery

# Use directly
synth-panel panel run --pack startup-founder --instrument pricing-discovery
```

**MCP equivalents:** `list_instrument_packs`, `save_instrument_pack`, `load_instrument_pack`. Mirrors the persona pack tools. The MCP server already has the infrastructure pattern — this is following an established convention, not inventing one.

**Why this matters:** Authoring a *good* multi-round (and now branching) instrument is the hard part. A novice user with `pricing-discovery`, `name-test`, `landing-page-comprehension`, `feature-prioritization-tradeoff` packs ready to install can do useful research on day one without designing an instrument from scratch. Persona packs answer "who am I asking?" — instrument packs answer "what should I ask them?" Both are needed for the flywheel to spin.

**Curation policy (CPO call):** A small `synth-panel/instruments` repo with hand-vetted packs ships at 0.5.0 launch — five instruments minimum, covering the highest-frequency indie-hacker use cases (name testing, pricing discovery, feature prioritization, landing page comprehension, churn diagnosis). Community contributions accepted via PR. We do not run a registry/marketplace — packs are git-installable, period. (See anti-scope.)

#### 3. Remove the Flat Output Format

0.4.0 deprecated the flat `results`/`synthesis` shape; 0.5.0 removes it. This was promised in EXECUTION-PLAN-040.md and the architect locked it. Single-round instruments now emit the same `rounds`-shaped output as multi-round (a one-element rounds list).

**Why this matters:** Two output shapes is technical debt. Every consumer of the MCP tool has to special-case "is this single-round or multi-round?" That branch should die before the API stabilizes for 1.0.0. 0.5.0 is the right release — far enough from 0.1.0 that users have had warning, close enough to 1.0.0 that the cleanup pays for itself.

**Migration:** A clear deprecation note in the 0.4.0 changelog; a `--legacy-output` flag in 0.5.0 that emits the old shape with a warning, removed in 0.6.0. Agents calling `run_panel` get the new shape unconditionally — agent traffic post-dates the deprecation, they were never relying on flat.

## What 0.5.0 is NOT

- **Not LLM-authored questions or routing.** Templates can reference synthesis data; routing predicates compare strings. The LLM does not generate the next question or pick the next round. Reproducibility and auditability are non-negotiable. (Anti-scope, settled.)
- **Not loops or back-edges.** Each round runs at most once. If a user wants to "go back to exploration with new framing," that's a new run.
- **Not persona-to-persona interaction.** Still independent panelists. Still settled.
- **Not a hosted instrument registry.** Packs are git-installable. We are not building a marketplace, ratings, comments, payments, or any of the SaaS-shaped surface that comes with one. (Anti-scope.)
- **Not a web UI for designing branching instruments.** Visualizing a DAG in YAML is not a problem we solve with a GUI. If anything, ship a `synth-panel instruments graph <file>` that emits a DOT/Mermaid diagram on stdout.
- **Not 1.0.0.** See below.

## Show HN Angle

0.4.0 was the planned Show HN feature (multi-round demo). If Show HN happened on 0.4.0, **0.5.0 is the "we listened, here's adaptive research" follow-up** — different audience, more depth. If Show HN was held, **0.5.0 is the stronger Show HN target**: branching instruments demo dramatically better than linear multi-round.

The 0.5.0 demo:

> "Run a research study where the questions adapt to what the panel actually says. Exploration round surfaces a pricing objection? The instrument routes to the pricing probe automatically. Surfaces an integration concern instead? Routes to the integration probe. Either way, you get a final synthesis at the end. One command. ~$0.20."

That's the demo where someone says "wait, you mean the *instrument itself* branches based on the synthesis?" Linear multi-round was impressive but explainable. Branching is the one that lands as obviously novel.

**My recommendation:** Hold Show HN for 0.5.0 unless the founder or community pressure forces a 0.4.0 launch. The marginal benefit is large.

## The Path to 1.0.0

The roadmap rule is "1.0.0 when someone has built something real on top and the API has been stable for 3+ minor releases." After 0.5.0 we will be **at** the threshold but **not past** it.

- API stability: 0.4.0 changed the output shape (added `rounds`). 0.5.0 removes the flat shape. So 0.5.0 → 0.6.0 → 0.7.0 are the three minor releases that need to ship without breaking changes before 1.0.0 is on the table.
- Real adoption: we need at least one external user shipping something that depends on synth-panel. Could be a research consultancy using it as an internal pre-screen. Could be an open-source product team with synth-panel in their CI as a "run the panel against this PR's copy changes" check. Could be an indie hacker referencing it in a launch post. We don't have this yet — Show HN is the most likely catalyst.

**0.6.0 will be a stabilization release.** No new features. Bug fixes, doc improvements, performance, expanded instrument library. The discipline of shipping a stabilization release is itself a signal that we respect the 1.0.0 contract.

**0.7.0 will be whatever the community asks for.** I am intentionally not committing to a 0.7.0 vision here. By that point we will have real user feedback and the right move is to listen, not to project.

## Architect Questions

These are product decisions the architect needs clarity on before designing 0.5.0:

1. **Predicate evaluation: pure string ops vs. minimal expression engine.** Branching predicates need `contains`, `equals`, `matches`. Should we hand-roll three evaluator functions (boring, safe, ~30 lines), or pull in a constrained expression library? **CPO recommendation:** Hand-roll. We control the surface area, no eval, no dependency, no surprises. If predicate complexity grows past three operators in a future release we can revisit.

2. **DAG validation timing.** When do we validate that the round graph is acyclic and that every `goto` resolves to a defined round? **CPO recommendation:** At instrument parse time, before any LLM calls. A bad instrument should fail in milliseconds with a clear error, not partway through a paid run. Reject cycles, forward-only references that don't resolve, and unreachable rounds (with a warning, not an error — unreachable rounds may be intentional during authoring).

3. **What happens when no `route_when` clause matches and there's no `else`?** **CPO recommendation:** Treat missing `else` as an authoring error and reject at parse time. Forcing every routing decision to have a default makes instruments more robust and forces authors to think about the edge case rather than fall through silently.

4. **Instrument pack format and discovery.** Persona packs are directories with a manifest. Should instrument packs be the same shape (directory + manifest) or single-file YAML? **CPO recommendation:** Single-file YAML. Instruments are typically one document; forcing a directory adds friction without value. The manifest goes inline as top-level keys (`name`, `version`, `description`, `author`). If a future instrument needs auxiliary files (e.g., reference materials), that's the moment to consider directories — not before.

5. **Path recording in MCP results.** Should the `path` array (which rounds ran, which predicates fired) be in the default response or behind a flag? **CPO recommendation:** Default. The path is core to reproducibility and audit. Hiding it behind a flag implies it's optional, which it isn't.

6. **Final synthesis when paths diverge.** With branching, different runs of the same instrument can take different paths. The final synthesis prompt needs to know which rounds actually ran. **CPO recommendation:** Pass only the rounds that actually executed to the final synthesis prompt — don't include skipped branches. The final synthesis describes what happened, not what could have happened.

## Disagreements I'd Pre-empt

I expect two pieces of pushback on this plan. Calling them out so we can have the argument now, not at execution time:

1. **"Branching is too complex for 0.5.0, let's do instrument library only."** No. The instrument library is the ecosystem play, but it loses most of its value if every instrument in the library is linear. The first 5 curated packs should *demonstrate* branching — `pricing-discovery` should branch on price-objection vs. value-objection, `name-test` should branch on positive-recall vs. negative-recall. Without branching the library is just YAML files. With branching the library is *authoring craft worth sharing*. They ship together.

2. **"Branching predicates should be more expressive — let users write Python lambdas / Jinja / JMESPath."** No. We deliberately picked the boring path in 0.4.0 templates and it has paid off in zero security issues, zero "what does this expression even mean" debugging, and a doc page that fits on a screen. Branching predicates use the same vocabulary. If a user needs more, they can author multiple instruments and route between them at the agent layer — that's exactly what `extend_panel` is for.

## Summary

0.5.0 is **the adaptive release**. Branching instruments make synth-panel choose its own research path based on what the panel says — the step beyond "linear rounds with templated questions." Instrument packs ship the second ecosystem flywheel, parallel to persona packs from 0.2.0. The flat output shape gets retired, paying down the deprecation debt before 1.0.0.

Branching is still the right bet. What's changed since the original roadmap is the framing: it's not "more sophistication on top of linear multi-round," it's "adaptive research in one tool call" — the deepening of agent-native value, not broadening. A single MCP call now offloads orchestration, persona memory, *and* research-design judgment to the harness. That is what makes synth-panel structurally different from manual prompting in a way that compounds with every release.

0.6.0 is a stabilization release. 0.7.0 is community-driven. 1.0.0 stays gated on real external adoption — Show HN (preferably on 0.5.0) is the most credible catalyst.

The 0.5.0 demo is the one that makes someone close their laptop and rewrite their next research plan. Ship it.
